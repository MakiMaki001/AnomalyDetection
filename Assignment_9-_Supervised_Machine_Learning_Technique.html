Running Random forest model

We will first run the model on all variables in the dataset (except date) without any over or under sampling technique.

The performance metrics we will be looking at are:

AUC: Measures the true positive rate in relation to the false positive rate. This metric seeks to show the performance of the. Standing for Area Under the Curve, it measures the area under the FP/(FP+TN)

MSE: Measures the squared error of the prediction per each point. This means that for each prediction, the measure calculates the difference between the predicting value and the targer variable of the model. It is a simple goodness of fit measure however prone to be heavily impacted by outliers.

RMSE: This is the square root of the MSE metric. In essence, it produces a similar measure of performance in that the metric shows the amount of error that the model has in relation to predicting the response variable. The square root however to scale the errors and therefore show a more normalized result.

LogLoss: Measures a binary (logarithmic) performance of the predictor to the response variable. It is a useful predictor to apply the uncertainty of the model to the overall dataset. This is one of the more effective measurments in logistic regression.

Gini: Measures the sensitivity of movement in a dataset. It is interpreted as somewhat similar to the inverse of the AUC measure. Commonly used in economics to determine inequality.

Conclusion

Sampling of the data has a direct effect on the overal performance of the AUC values created by the model. The difference in AUC results from undersampling to oversampling of the datasets can cause a 6.7% change in the AUC score, and 2.8% just from undersampling (difference from no over or under sampling techniques). Therefore, while it is important to get the exact correct sampling ratio on an imbalanced dataset, in order to get a higher score than .67 of AUC on the response variable,the features used will need more engineering on them.

The sampling ratio that produced the best results is 1 to 3, which aligns closesly with the 0.2400794 positive response to overall observation percentage. It is surprising that the 1 to 4 ratio (0.6390547) did not have a high score.

The range in AUC values different models ran was between:

##AUC

Best Overall

0.6769257: Created in grid with 150 ntrees and max depth of 8

Highest sample

0.6600162: 1 to 3 sampled data

No imbalance in sample

0.6388367: No under or oversampling attempted on data

Smallest AUC from oversampling

0.5943416: Oversampling of the response variable my a margin of 9 to 1

The other performance metrics were interesting to interpret in their variability. All of these additional metrics had an interesting impact.

MSE and RMSE has best metrics in the 1 to 3 model which also included the best AUC model. This was differed than the performance of the most extreme metrics (where 1 to 9 sample ratios were used), the dataset with the best MSE and RMSE metrics were the oversampleped data.

Logloss really highlighted the necessity for more feature engineering in regards to this dataset. Logloss for the models ran was consistently above 1. Even for lowest logloss value- .62, does not indicate that of a succesful model in predicting default.

The gini measure correlated relatively strongly with the performance of the different sampled data however there was a lot more variance in the value of them.
